Title: The Tragic Portrayal of Clytemnestra in Aeschylus’ Oresteia
Introduction:
Aeschylus’ Oresteia, a trilogy comprising Agamemnon, The Libation Bearers (Chophorai), and Electra (Elektra), explores the devastating cycle of revenge, justice, and the enduring consequences of familial betrayal. At the heart of this trilogy lies Clytemnestra, a figure whose complex portrayal as a character evolves across the three plays, reflecting her transformation from a tragic wife to a vengeful avenger and, ultimately, a victim of the same violence she sought to escape. This essay aims to analyze Clytemnestra’s characterization in detail, examining her roles, motivations, and the broader themes of vengeance, justice, and tragedy.
Clytemnestra’s portrayal is multifaceted, drawing parallels with other tragic figures in Greek literature, such as Medea, Phaedra, and, in later interpretations, even Hamlet’s queen. Her journey through the trilogy mirrors the timeless struggle between personal morality and the demands of justice, while also challenging traditional gender roles within the context of ancient Greek society.
Agamemnon: The Tragic Figure
In Agamemnon, Clytemnestra is introduced as a tragic figure caught between her duty to her family and her growing resentment toward her husband, Agamemnon. When Agamemnon returns from the Trojan War with the captive Cassandra, Clytemnestra’s jealousy is awakened, not least because of Cassiope, the daughter of Chryseides, whom Agamemnon has taken as a concubine. This act of betrayal is compounded by Agamemnon’s refusal to bathe after his return from war, which he claims is a purification ritual for his troops but which Clytemnestra interprets as an affront to her, the legitimate queen.
Clytemnestra’s silence in the face of Agamemnon’s actions can be interpreted in two ways: as complicity or as a passive acceptance of her fate. On one hand, her refusal to wash Agamemnon could be seen as an attempt to maintain some sense of dignity and honor within the household. On the other hand, it can be viewed as a symbolic act of submission to her husband’s authority, which ultimately contributes to his demise.
The chorus in Agamemnon provides insight into Clytemnestra’s guilt, suggesting that her silence is not mere complicity but an active participation in Agamemnon’s destruction. By refusing to intervene or wash her husband, she allows the so-called “pollution” to spread, which, according to Greek beliefs, would necessitate his death. This interpretation aligns with scholarly views that Clytemnestra’s actions are rooted not just in jealousy but in a deeper sense of justice and retribution.
The Libation Bearers: A Vengeful Transformation
In The Libation Bearers, Clytemnestra undergoes a dramatic transformation, evolving from a passive participant in Agamemnon’s downfall to an active avenger. This shift is central to understanding her character development and the broader themes of the trilogy. After Agamemnon’s death, Clytemnestra returns to Argos with Orestes, who has already killed his father in an act of vengeance. Now, she becomes a catalyst for further violence, manipulating Orestes into killing both Cassiope and her own mother, Cytemnestra (a name that is shared with herself, adding layers of irony).
Clytemnestra’s justification for these actions lies in her perception of Agamemnon’s betrayal and the subsequent dishonor brought upon their household. She frames her vengeance as a form of justice, arguing that she is not the aggressor but the avenger of her husband’s crimes. This justification mirrors the rhetoric of Orestes, who claims his violence is a form of dike (retribution). However, both characters’ actions ultimately perpetuate the cycle of violence they seek to resolve.
The role of the Furies in this play is crucial to understanding Clytemnestra’s transformation. The Furies are divine figures who embody justice and retribution, but their presence in Clytemnestra’s home suggests that her actions are both justified and necessitated by Agamemnon’s transgressions. By aligning herself with the Furies, Clytemnestra situates herself as a vengeful instrument of justice, rather than an outright villain.
Electra: The Tragedy of Revenge
In Electra, the final play of the trilogy, Clytemnestra faces the consequences of her actions. Her decision to participate in Orestes’ revenge has left her isolated and morally compromised, unable to find redemption or peace. Electra, Agamemnon’s daughter, now serves as a catalyst for the play’s tragic events, as she attempts to avenge her father by killing Clytemnestra.
Clytemnestra’s destruction in this play is both inevitable and ironic. Her attempt to avenge herself has led to her own downfall, creating a cycle of violence that mirrors Agamemnon’s initial betrayal. This reflects the broader theme of hamartia (blindness or moral failure) that plagues the characters of the Oresteia. Clytemnestra’s actions are driven by a sense of justice and righteousness, yet they ultimately lead to her own destruction, highlighting the tragic nature of her character.
Clytemnestra’s final moments in Electra are poignant. Despite her participation in Orestes’ revenge, she is portrayed as both a victim and a perpetrator of violence. Her death at the hands of Electra and Orestes symbolizes the waste of her potential, as her actions have not only destroyed her family but also eroded any sense of moral integrity.
Themes of Vengeance and Justice
Clytemnestra’s journey through the Oresteia raises important questions about vengeance, justice, and the nature of morality. Her initial justification for revenge is rooted in a sense of injustice and betrayal, yet her actions ultimately escalate the violence she sought to resolve. This creates a tension between personal morality and the demands of retribution.
Clytemnestra’s portrayal also challenges traditional gender roles in ancient Greek tragedy. While she is often framed as a victim of Agamemnon’s transgressions, her active participation in his downfall complicates this interpretation. She is not merely a passive sufferer but an agent of change, even if that change leads to further violence.
In contemporary interpretations, Clytemnestra has been compared to other tragic heroines, such as Medea and Phaedra, who also grapple with issues of loyalty, justice, and revenge. Her story reflects the universal struggle between individual morality and societal expectations, a theme that resonates across cultural and temporal boundaries.
Comparisons and Influences
Clytemnestra’s character has influenced numerous interpretations in literature, theater, and philosophy. In Sophocles’ Electra, for example, Clytemnestra is portrayed as a figure of shame and guilt, reflecting the moral ambiguity of her actions. Later writers and directors have drawn parallels with characters such as Hamlet’s queen, who is torn between loyalty and retribution.
In the Oresteia itself, Clytemnestra’s transformation from a tragic wife to a vengeful mother reflects the idea that justice can be a double-edged sword, capable of both avenging wrongs and perpetuating violence. This theme is central to the trilogy’s exploration of the human condition.
Conclusion:
Clytemnestra’s portrayal in Aeschylus’ Oresteia is a rich and multifaceted exploration of vengeance, justice, and morality. Her journey from a tragic wife to a vengeful avenger to a victim of her own actions underscores the complexity of human behavior in the face of betrayal and injustice. While her ultimate destruction serves as a cautionary tale about the consequences of unchecked anger, it also highlights the enduring struggle between personal morality and societal expectations.
Clytemnestra’s story continues to resonate in contemporary discussions of justice and revenge, offering insights into the universal human experience of seeking retribution while grapples with the moral implications of their actions. Her character remains a powerful lens through which to examine the complexities of power, gender, and violence in ancient and modern societies alike.

Final Answer
The role of Clytemnestra in Aeschylus' Oresteia is best summarized by her transformation from a tragic figure to an avenger, illustrating the complex interplay between justice, revenge, and morality. Her story reflects the tension between personal ethics and societal expectations, making her a profound exploration of human behavior.
